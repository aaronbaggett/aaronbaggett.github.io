Qualitative IDEA Data

Student evaluations of teaching are notoriously chided among faculty members, with evidence suggesting their use in evaluating faculty should be limited, if not disregarded. However, I often find the qualitative portions of my SET results to be the most enlightening and usefulgood and bad. Therefore, I find myself gleaning more from the qualitative feedback. Here, the students can tell me what they liked and did not like about the course. It is much easier to revise course content, organization, assessments, etc. armed with student feedback than it is with a mysterious discipline-adjusted composite score. Using qualitative IDEA data in the section below I address areas of strength and opportunity applied to my classroom teaching.

Strengths

  1. Commitment to Student Success

Dr.Baggett is a great professor and cares a lot about his students and their learning. He is great at making sure how he is teaching is working for the class, and is more than willing to change how he teaches if the class is having difficulties. I have taken previous courses with him, and I was happy to see that he taught this course as well. All in all a great teacher.

My consistent commitment to student success is often met with appreciation from students (see links to IDEA reports above). For me, it is not an option. I believe a commitment to student success should be the minimum and default burden of any good teacher. During the point of every mid-semester I try and send each student a personalized email with a progress report while either encouraging them to continue working hard or offering suggestions for how to improve. These seem to be very well received and effective.

  1. Enthusiasm and Humor

Dr.Baggett made class fun and interesting. His constant humor made me want to attend class and learn. He does a grand job at grasping your attention on the boring topics and pushing you to try harder. Engaging us in real life situations and applying those to statistics made it more realistic and more interesting to learn because I felt like it targeted me where I am with my interests.

Understanding the dread most students experience when they find themselves in a statistics course, I try to introduce humor as often as I can in my lectures. Humor can be very effective in the classroom. I would even argue especially in a statistics classroom. Humor can give everyone a chance to exhale. In fact, here: KNOCK KNOCK! Well, never mind. I should probably conserve my word count. Feel free to swing by one of our class meetings. Fair warning though, you might want to bring a light jacket or sweater. I like to keep my classroom on the chilly side. Otherwise, I would have no way of keeping my comedy fresh.

Opportunities

  1. More Student-Friendly Software Requirements

RStudio is the worst. Truly. I havent really understood the connection between R and what weve learned in class. I also feel like i have to mostly teach myself a lot of what i need to know for the exams from the textbook. I really feel like the class could be more doable and more understandable if we didnt use R. Dr.Baggett is an excellent and brilliant professor. Its just R that makes this class difficult.

In PSYC 2316, I require students to use and learn the statistical software application R. R is a free, open source statistical programming language. As a statistical programming language, R offers greater flexibility and power compared to other statistical software applications. Being open source allows for statisticians and scientists across the world to contribute to the R framework, ensuring users have access to a comprehensive set of tools available to solve a variety of problems. Since R is considered a command line application we use the RStudio graphical user interface (GUI) to interact with and control R. RStudio is a highly functional GUI with the ability to produce dynamic reports with in-line code and output built right in. Used by companies like Walmart, Liberty Mutual, and Accenture, to name a few, RStudio is an industry standard when it comes to data analysis.

R is not an easy language to grasp and the learning curve is particularly steepespecially while also learning statistics for the first time. I realize that, but believe it is becoming more and more the standard tool used in quantitative social science graduate programs. Therefore, students need to learn it. I can work harder to produce more interactive tutorials for students that are accessible beyond in-class troubleshooting sessions.

  1. Connecting Analyses to Content

The R program we used in class seemed like it didnt have anything to do with what the course was about. I really hated that we had to be in teams to do them because not every team member put in the same amount of effort as the others. The labs just seemed to be more of a hassle than anything else.

Related to the above section on the challenges of using R, I could admittedly do a better job of connecting the course content to the corresponding statistical analyses we work on during the semester. As part of PSYC 2316, I require students to complete a total of seven individual lab assignments. In general, all lab assignments I assign are related to the course content. However, given the way in which I have written and structured each assignment, it would not be unreasonable for some students to see these are simply standalone assignments with little connection to the textbook. As a result, I plan to revise and rework all lab assignments for implementation during the Fall 2021 semester. Additionally, I plan to revise and rework all lectures to include more coverage of essential concepts with corresponding in-class R integration.
